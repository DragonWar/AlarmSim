I was just thinking about my car's battery as I drove home today. I haven't changed it during the life of my car - and I got the car about four years ago. I don't know how old the battery was when I got the car, either (I wasn't the first owner).

I know batteries wear out - and when they do it's pretty awful because it typically means you can't start your car. But your car should also be able to predict this - by doing things like monitoring the ESR of the battery and how that changes over the course of the lifetime of the pack.

So why don't any cars do this? It sure as hell sounds easy to me. And it'd mean avoiding the whole car not starting thing.

Oh yeah - and what's the typical lifetime of a car's battery?

I don't know what the life is in Boston Massachusetts, but here in the hot desert it's five or six years, and you don't get any warning. It seems perfectly fine when you park your car one evening, and in the morning the battery is dead and won't take a charge. It's happened to me more than once.

Many years ago I could tell when the battery was nearing death. They seemed to lose one cell. The voltage measured 10 volts. The engine cranked slowly. When the enging was running, the ammeter on the dashboard would show abnormally high charging current. But I could use the battery for a week or two or more, at least in the summer.

But no more. I've had two batteries die on my present car with no warning. One minute the engine cranked normally, 30 minutes later there was nothing. When I opened the door with the key in the ignition, I heard boing, boing, boing. It was the "chime" I normally hear in such circumstances, but it sounded strange. I turned on the radio but it didn't work, and the boing stopped. After that, nothing.

I got a jump start and drove home. I had one of the portable jump start battery packs so I knew I could start it. The next morning I measured the battery voltage at 12 volts. I turned on the lights and it dropped to one volt.

Car batteries are not really suitable as backup battery anyway, the electronics that stay active probably consume less then the self-discharge. Things like remote doorlocks shutdown the radio receiver after two weeks, at least on some cars.

Some batteries come with a built-in specific gravity meter, which is the green/red/absent dot you can see in a little clear round window.

Some very high end cars have electronic charging circuits to lengthen the life of the battery, instead of the plain simple alternator with crude voltage regulator. So I heard.
